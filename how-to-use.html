How to use UniChat

Your campus assistant with citations

UniChat helps you find and understand program, course, and university information using connected institutional sources. When accuracy matters, check the citations and follow the link back to the original page or document.

✅ Citations you can verify
🔒 Respects your access (RBAC)
🧭 Better questions = better answers

Quick start

Quick start (30 seconds)

1) Ask one clear question. 2) Read the answer. 3) Open the citations to verify. Then ask a follow-up like “show the policy section” or “summarize in 5 bullets.”

Good prompts to copy

• “What are the prerequisites for IRM4005? Cite the official source.”
• “Summarize the late penalty policy in 5 bullets, with citations.”
• “Compare these two sources and highlight differences.”

Tip If the answer looks uncertain or missing details, ask UniChat to “search again and cite every major claim.” If sources conflict, ask it to cite both and explain which is most official.

What UniChat is best for

  • Finding official info faster (program requirements, prerequisites, deadlines, forms, office pages, policies)
  • Explaining complex documents in plain language (while citing the relevant sections)
  • Comparing sources (what changed, what differs, which is newest)
  • Step-by-step guidance for common processes (with sources for each step)

How UniChat works

UniChat typically (1) retrieves relevant passages from connected sources, then (2) generates a response from that evidence. That’s why citations matter: they show where the answer came from so you can verify it.

What if UniChat can’t find something?
Try adding context (course code, term, office name). If it still can’t find it, ask: “What sources did you check, and what keywords did you use?” If it’s not documented publicly, UniChat should say so rather than guessing.

Ask better questions

Include key context whenever possible:

  • Course code (example: IRM4005)
  • Program (example: BIT IRM)
  • Term/year (example: Winter 2026)
  • Office/unit (example: Registrar, Library, Department)
  • Desired format (bullets, steps, short summary, comparison)
Examples
• “Explain [policy] in plain language and cite the exact section.”
• “Give me a step-by-step checklist for [process] with sources for each step.”
• “If there are conflicting sources, cite both and tell me which one appears most official.”

Understanding citations

  • Strong citations usually mean UniChat found clear supporting evidence.
  • Thin or missing citations is a sign to request a re-check or a narrower question.
  • For assignments or official decisions, open the citation and confirm the wording yourself.

Access & privacy

UniChat respects role-based access. You’ll only see what you’re allowed to view. Avoid pasting sensitive personal information (student number, grades, passwords, medical/financial info).

If you think you should have access
Make sure you’re signed in with the right account. If access still seems wrong, contact your course staff or the office that manages the resource.

Troubleshooting

“I cannot find that”
Add more keywords (course code, term, office). Then ask: “Search broader and list the top 3 relevant sources with citations.”
“This seems outdated”
Ask: “Use the most recent source available, tell me the date, and cite it.”
“The answer is too long”
Ask: “Give me a 3-bullet summary, then a detailed version.” Or: “Max 8 bullets.”

Feedback

If you see broken citations, missing sources, or unclear answers, leave feedback (when available). Helpful feedback includes the question you asked, what you expected, and what UniChat returned.
